# NVIDIA Corporation (NVDA) — Equity Research

**Rating: REDUCE / SELL · Intrinsic value ≈ $240 · Price $409 (10 Jun 2023) · ~40% downside to value**

NVIDIA is a genuinely exceptional business that, at $409, is priced for a future in
which almost everything goes right. An upbeat intrinsic valuation — dominant share
of a fast-growing AI-chip market, operating margins climbing to 40%, capital
efficiency rising to the industry norm — produces a value of roughly $240 per share,
about 40% below the current price. Across a simulation of the key assumptions the
stock trades near the 95th percentile of the value distribution: a bull case exists,
but the odds embedded in the price are poor. The rating is a reflection of price, not
of the company; the franchise is one of the best in technology.

## The semiconductor business

The chip industry, in its current form, was built by the PC revolution of the 1980s
and has since matured. Aggregate semiconductor revenue surged through the 1990s, then
settled into single-digit growth from 2001 to 2020 as rising chip demand was offset by
eroding pricing power. Profitability has stayed healthy but cyclical, and reported
margins understate the truth, because accounting treats R&D — the industry's central
investment — as an operating expense rather than capitalizing it. Leadership rotates
ruthlessly: of the top ten chipmakers in 1990, only Intel and Texas Instruments remain
on the 2023 list, with TSMC now at the top. That history is a standing warning against
assuming any incumbent, however dominant today, holds its position for a decade.

## NVIDIA: the opportunist

NVIDIA's distinction is not size — its revenue still trails Intel's and TSMC's — but
the consistency with which it has found high-margin growth in a maturing industry.
Founded in 1993, it spent years as a niche graphics-card maker before well-timed bets
on gaming and crypto lifted revenue growth above 27% in 2016–2020, with gross and
operating margins running ahead of the peer group. Two features of the model matter
for valuation. First, NVIDIA is fabless: it designs chips and outsources production to
TSMC, so its margins come from pricing power rather than manufacturing scale — and its
supply chain is exposed to any disruption around Taiwan. Second, growth has not been
capital-light. Capitalizing R&D and recomputing returns, the business generated about
65 cents of revenue per dollar of capital invested in 2022, well below the industry
median; NVIDIA has invested heavily to reach its present position. Its rise to a
trillion-dollar market capitalization has also not been smooth — the stock lost more
than 80% of its value in 2002 and 2008, and more than 50% in 2018. Even the best
franchises hand value investors periodic entry points.

## The AI driver

The case for NVIDIA rests on artificial intelligence, and here the company has
substance where most have only narrative: it already earns AI revenue and holds an
estimated ~80% share of a market for AI-optimized data-center chips that was perhaps
$15bn in 2022. The debate is about how large that market becomes. Estimates for the
AI-chip market in 2030 range from roughly $200bn to $300bn, and NVIDIA enters that
decade with a clear lead over AMD, Intel and the hyperscalers' custom silicon. The
bull case is that AI becomes the growth engine that replaces maturing gaming and crypto
demand, with automotive computing as a second leg. The discipline a valuation imposes
is to ask not whether that story is *possible* — it plainly is — but whether the price
already assumes it is *certain*.

## Story to numbers

Translating that narrative into the four drivers of value, on R&D-adjusted financials:
revenue is built bottom-up from the AI, automotive and gaming segments, with the
addressable markets sized and a defensible (dominant in AI, growing in auto) share
assigned, declining from roughly 50% in the first year toward the riskfree rate by
year ten and reaching about $267bn of revenue by 2033. The R&D-adjusted operating
margin recovers from a 2022 setback toward a 40% target by 2027 and holds — a premium
to peers, but within NVIDIA's own history (it earned 42.5% in 2020). Reinvestment
efficiency improves as scale pays off, with sales-to-capital rising from 0.65 toward
the industry median of 1.15. Risk is captured by a cost of capital that starts at the
US semiconductor average of 12.21% during the high-growth phase and drifts to the
mature market average of 8.85%, with NVIDIA's persistently low effective tax rate
(~15%) reflecting its IP structure.

This produces an intrinsic value of about **$240 per share**, against the $409 price.
Roughly 77% of that value sits beyond the explicit ten-year window, in the terminal
value — a reminder that this is, at bottom, a bet on the durability of NVIDIA's
franchise long after the current AI surge. The result is not fragile to a single
optimistic input: it is the *combination* of dominant AI share, super-normal margins
and improving capital efficiency that yields $240, and the price still sits well above
it.

## What the price is pricing

Inverting the valuation is more revealing than the point estimate. Holding the other
drivers at their base case, the $409 price implies year-ten revenue of roughly $489bn —
a 34% ten-year compound rate, and 83% above an already-aggressive base case — or,
alternatively, an operating margin near 65%, against a 40% base and a 50% level that
would already stretch the limits of the chip business. Neither is impossible; both sit
beyond the optimistic story. A two-variable breakeven table makes the joint requirement
concrete: justifying the price needs a daunting combination of extraordinary revenue
*and* super-normal margins.

| Year-10 revenue ＼ operating margin | 40% | 50% |
|---|---|---|
| $300bn | $262 | $338 |
| $400bn | $340 | $441 ✓ |

## The odds, not just the estimate

Because every one of these inputs is uncertain, the honest output is a distribution
rather than a number. Running the four drivers as distributions — year-ten revenue
spanning under $100bn to over $600bn, the operating margin triangular between 30% and
50%, sales-to-capital between 0.65 and 1.94, and the cost of capital between 8.85% and
15% — yields a simulated median value of about $238, almost exactly the base case. The
$409 price falls at roughly the **94th percentile** of that distribution, with only a
~5% chance the stock is worth more than its price on these assumptions. The market is
not pricing a plausible outcome; it is pricing one of the better ones.

A relative-valuation cross-check points the same way while exposing its own limits.
On trailing multiples, NVIDIA's semiconductor peers imply a value of only $40–115 per
share — far below both price and intrinsic value — because trailing comparables ignore
exactly the growth the discounted-cash-flow model is built to capture. The lesson is
not that NVIDIA is worth $40, but that the entire bull case lives in the future, not in
what the company earns today.

## Judgment

The investing decision follows from the gap between value and price. An intrinsic value
of ~$240 against a $409 price, with the price near the 95th percentile of a simulated
value distribution, is a sell signal for a value-driven investor — and notably, the
gap survives an honest bull case rather than depending on a bearish one. The pragmatic
path, and the one Damodaran took, is to sell into the strength while retaining a partial
position for the optionality that NVIDIA finds yet another market to dominate, as it
has before. Trading is a separate game: momentum can carry the price higher for weeks or
months, and an investor trading the stock should watch for shifts in that momentum
rather than for value. What the valuation rules out is only the comfortable belief that
the current price reflects a base-case future. It does not.

## What would change this view

The thesis is wrong if NVIDIA finds a second and third AI-scale market in which it can
wield dominant share — the revenue path to justifying the price requires roughly that —
or if AI economics let it sustain operating margins above 50% durably, which would be
unprecedented in the industry. The principal risks on the other side are the
fabless dependence on TSMC and Taiwan, the entry of AMD and hyperscaler custom silicon
into the AI-chip market, and the cyclicality that has repeatedly humbled even the
strongest semiconductor names. The valuation is most sensitive to the terminal
assumptions and the cost of capital; a reader who disagrees with those should re-run the
model, which is the point of shipping it.

### Price Target Methodology

**Method 1 — Discounted Cash Flow (DCF):** *Primary.* Free cash flows to the firm
were projected over an explicit 10-year forecast period (2023–2033) and discounted at
a cost of capital starting at 12.21% (US semiconductor average) and drifting to 8.85%
(mature market average). The terminal value was estimated using a perpetual growth
model with a terminal growth rate anchored at the risk-free rate. Accounting
adjustments applied: R&D capitalization, operating lease capitalization, and
stock-based compensation expensed. DCF-derived intrinsic value: **$240 per share**.

**Method 2 — Reverse DCF / Market-Implied:** *Secondary.* The market price of $409
was inverted to solve for the growth rate and operating margin the price implies. The
price-implied year-10 revenue of ~$489bn (34% CAGR) and/or operating margin near 65%
were assessed against NVIDIA's history, semiconductor industry economics, and
competitive dynamics, and found to price outcomes beyond the plausible base case.

**Method 3 — Monte Carlo Simulation:** *Supporting.* The four valuation drivers were
modeled as distributions (10,000 trials). The simulated median value of ~$238
confirmed the base case, and the $409 price fell at the 94th percentile of the value
distribution.

| Method | Value Range ($/share) | Weight |
|--------|----------------------|--------|
| DCF (primary) | $240 | 60% |
| Reverse DCF / Market-Implied | Impractical at current price | 15% |
| Monte Carlo Simulation | $48–$543 (P10–P90) | 25% |
| **Intrinsic Value** | **~$240** | 100% |

**Triangulation:** The final intrinsic value of ~$240 per share reflects a synthesis
of the above three methods, with primary weight (60%) on the DCF which is best suited
to NVIDIA's growth-stage profile, and supporting weights on the market-implied and
simulation approaches which confirm the DCF result from different angles.

**Valuation horizon:** 12 months from the date of this report. **Key assumptions
that carry the result:** terminal growth rate, target operating margin (40%), and
cost of capital trajectory.
